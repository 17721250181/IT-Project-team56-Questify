Product Overview 

Questify is an AI-assisted, student-driven question bank system developed for the Object-Oriented Software Development (OOSD) subject at the University of Melbourne. The system aims to make students more engaged with the subject throughout the semester, addressing long-standing issues of passive learning and last-minute exam cramming.Teaching staff can review content and monitor activity through lightweight admin views.

Across three sprints, we delivered an end-to-end workflow: sign-in, question creation for both MCQ and short answer, browsing/searching, attempting questions with model answers/explanations, commenting/rating, leaderboard and admin review. The system runs on React + Vite (frontend) and Django + Django REST Framework (backend), deployed to Render.

How Questify Works

In Questify, students take an active role as both learners and content creators:

  • Students design their own questions based on weekly topics, provide answers, and write explanations.

  • Their peers can attempt these questions, rate their quality, and leave comments for discussion.

  • The system thus transforms question practice into an interactive and reflective learning process: students not only solve problems but also evaluate and improve each other’s work.

  • A leaderboard and reward system further motivates ongoing engagement by recognising students for the quality and creativity of their contributions.

  • The teaching team moderates submitted questions to ensure academic accuracy and maintaining content quality.

This approach aligns with the client’s goal of “making students more engaging to the subject” and introduces a sustainable, student-led enhancement to the OOSD learning experience.

Core Users and Journeys

  • Student (creator): Create a question → submit → review AI explanation → receive admin feedback

  • Student (solver): Browse/filter questions → attempt → compare with both given and AI solution/explanation → comment/rate/save

  • Teaching staff (admin): Review new questions → approve/reject with notes → check lightweight activity views.

Requirements 

Evolution of Requirements

Our client designed this subject back in the early 2000s. Since then, new languages, frameworks, and especially AI have completely changed how students learn to code. She wanted to redesign the subject to make it more engaging and interactive, but didn’t have a fixed idea. She was open to anything that could improve participation and motivation.

After the first briefing, we created a short survey for past-year students to find out what they wanted most. The most common answer was “an AI platform that can generate unlimited practice questions for exam revision.” So our initial idea was to build an AI-powered question bank.

However, after discussing with our tutor and client, we realised this went against her goal. It focused on passive practice rather than real engagement. Then we held several group meetings and decided to shift towards our current idea, A student-led question platform where students create, attempt, and review each other’s questions. This direction better fits the client’s aim of making learning more interactive and student-driven.

User Story

After aligning our direction with client, we first create Who/Do/Be/Feel list and then mapped the main use cases into user stories. We organised user stories by epics to emphasise a student-led workflow and minimise teaching effort.

  • 1. Student Profile & History

  • 2. Sign-Up & Authentication

  • 3. Creating & Posting Questions

  • 4. Solving & Saving

  • 5. Browse & Discovery

  • 6. Administration

  • 7. Leaderboard

Functional Requirements

Based on previous document we agreed on the following requirements:

  • Login / Register

    • Register with university email (format validation).

    • Login and logout with username and password.

    • Password reset

  • Profile

    • View and edit profile information (name and avatar).

    • See all created questions and their current review status.

    • View attempt history with results and timestamps.

    • View saved questions list

    • Activity heat map shows daily attempts and engagement across the semester.

  • Question Creation

    • Choose question type: Multiple-Choice or Short Answer.

    • Fill in question stem, answer, and explanation.

    • Tag questions by week or topic.

    • AI-assisted explanation generation for short-answer questions.

  • Question Solving

    • Attempt questions and submit answers and view correct answer + AI explanation after submission.

    • Record attempt results for progress tracking.

    • Comment and rate the question for feedback.

  • Question List

    • Browse all questions in the list

    • See each question’s information (e.g. current status)

    • Filter by week or topic (aligned with OOSD schedule).

    • Search bar for keyword or tag search.

    • Sort questions by time, rating or times of attempts.

    • Bookmark questions to save for later.

  • Comments & Discussion

    • Post comments under each question.

    • Reply and like comments for discussion.

  • Leaderboard Page

    • Rankings are categorized by daily, weekly, monthly, and overall time frames based on points.

    • Display the current student’s ranking for each time frame.

    • Highlight the Top 3 students for every time frame.

    • Provide a full leaderboard list for each time frame.

  • Admin

    • Review and verify student-created questions.

    • Approve or reject with feedback notes.

    • Visualise student activity.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Performance

    • System loads and responds to most user actions quickly in 3s.

    • Supports up to 500 concurrent users without major slowdown.

    • Core data operations (open, save, switch pages) are near-instant and visible to others within 1 min.

    • Designed for horizontal scaling and load balancing.

  • Reliability

    • Maintains 99.9% uptime during service hours.

    • < 1% failure rate for critical operations (login, save, search).

    • Clear error messages and automatic recovery mechanisms.

  • Security

    • Passwords encrypted (bcrypt), MFA for admins, role-based access control.

    • Only @unimelb.edu.au accounts allowed.

  • Usability

    • New users can sign up and start within 5 min; core actions within 3 clicks.

    • Simple, user-friendly flows with inline validation and clear feedback.

  • Compatibility

    • Responsive across desktop, tablet, and mobile.

    • Works consistently on Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and Edge.

Front-end Design

Front-End Overview

The front-end of Questify was designed to provide students with a seamless, motivating, and visually consistent learning environment. The interface supports a full end-to-end journey—from logging in to creating, attempting, and reviewing questions—without ever leaving the single-page application.
Our design goal was to make interaction feel lightweight and purposeful, reducing the friction between engagement and study. The color palette adopts the University of Melbourne’s academic blue tones, complemented by white and muted accent colors to maintain readability and focus. Layouts, icons, and typography are kept minimal, following a consistent design language across all views.

Core Interaction Flow

  • Global Navigation Bar: Present across all authenticated pages, providing quick access to Home, Question List, Create Question, Leaderboard, and Profile. The top-right dropdown allows users to log out or access their profile page.

  • Feedback & Validation System: Inline validation, toast notifications, and success prompts are consistently applied for all user actions—login, question submission, or rating—to enhance user confidence and minimize confusion.

  • Responsive Layout: The React-based UI automatically adapts to different screen sizes, ensuring functionality remains intact across desktop and tablet displays.

Together, these elements ensure that users can transition smoothly between stages of learning (creating, solving, reflecting) while maintaining awareness of where they are within the system.

Design Principles and Consistency

  • Clarity over Complexity: Each page prioritizes essential functions and removes unnecessary visual clutter. For instance, the Question List and Question Page use collapsible or paginated views to prevent information overload.

  • Continuity of Experience: Shared components such as buttons, filters, and search bars retain identical appearance and placement across pages. This continuity allows users to focus on their learning rather than interface changes.

  • Feedback and Guidance: From subtle hover effects to visible status indicators (e.g., “Pending”, “Approved”, “Rejected”), the interface keeps users informed about the state of their contributions without requiring them to check manually.

Accessibility was also considered from the outset. All color choices pass contrast checks, form inputs are labeled for screen readers.

Page Design

  • Login & Register: A split-screen design introducing the platform’s purpose alongside minimal input forms. The emphasis is on simplicity, readability, and error-free entry.

  • Home Page: Provides orientation and entry points to recent activities, saved questions, and leaderboard highlights. It acts as a motivational dashboard rather than a static landing page.

  • Question List & Discovery: A searchable and filterable database of all approved questions. Filters (week, topic, keyword) and sorting options are always visible, enabling quick access to relevant content.

  • Question Page: Focuses on readability and engagement. Clear typographic hierarchy separates the question, options, explanations, and comment sections. Color cues (green for correct, red for incorrect) reinforce learning feedback visually.

  • Post Question: A structured form with guided sections (Question Type, Content, Answer, Explanation, Tags). Dynamic field validation ensures completeness before submission, promoting consistent data quality.

  • Leaderboard: Uses a visually clean ranking list with badge highlights for the top three students. The tone is friendly and motivating rather than competitive, aligning with the platform’s educational focus.

  • Profile Page: Presents activity history, saved questions, and a GitHub-style heatmap representing engagement across the semester—turning learning data into a visual summary of effort and consistency.

  • Admin Page: Offers teaching staff a lightweight management interface with approval buttons, color-coded statuses, and summary metrics for quick review.

Design Evolution and User-Centered Considerations

Throughout development, UI decisions were continuously refined through internal feedback loops and client review sessions. Early prototypes emphasized functionality, while later iterations focused on polish and guidance. We introduced tooltips, refined spacing, and consistent iconography.
Testing confirmed that first-time users could navigate the platform and complete core actions (login, post, attempt) within a few minutes, aligning with our usability goals.

Future Improvements

While the current design provides a cohesive experience, future iterations could enhance interactivity and accessibility:

  • University Single Sign-On (SSO) to simplify authentication and improve security.

  • Mobile optimization for smaller screens and touch input.

  • Visual gamification, such as badges or streak counters integrated into the leaderboard and profile views.

  • Adaptive theming (dark/light modes) for improved user comfort.

Architectural Design 

High-Level Architecture

For details of each component, please refer to https://chma-unimelb-student.atlassian.net/wiki/x/WIUSAg.

Backend Modules & Responsibilities

The backend is organised into modular Django apps:

  • users: authentication, session and CSRF handling, and user profiles (managing profile pictures).

  • questions: question creating, tagging, AI explanation, comment, like and reply system.

  • attempts: attempt logging, user activity, and result display.

  • admin: admin verification workflows for question approval/rejection and summarising platform data.

  • leaderboard: point calculation and ranking aggregation.

Each module communicates via Django ORM models and REST API serializers, ensuring clear data ownership and minimal coupling.

Frontend Responsibilities

The frontend is a React (Vite) application organized into feature-based directories.

It communicates with the Django backend through Axios API clients, and maintains global authentication state via React Context (AuthContext.jsx).

The UI enables smooth navigation across question creation, solving, commenting, profile, and leaderboard pages without full page reloads.

CSRF tokens and session cookies are automatically managed.

API Endpoints

To fully understand how backend and frontend communicate, please refer to https://chma-unimelb-student.atlassian.net/wiki/x/A4BMAg for details.

Key Architectural Decisions (ADRs)

Decision Area

Chosen Approach

Rationale / Trade-offs

  1. Framework Stack

Django REST Framework + React (Vite)

Adopted a clear separation of concerns: Django provides RESTful APIs, authentication, and persistence; React handles interactive UI and routing. This ensures modularity and easier parallel development between backend and frontend.

  1. Authentication Method

Django session-based authentication with CSRF protection

Uses Django's session authentication with HttpOnly cookies for security and immediate logout capability. CSRF tokens managed automatically via axios interceptors.

  1. Database

SQLite for development; PostgreSQL for production

SQLite’s zero-configuration setup allowed simple local iteration. The schema and ORM design are compatible with PostgreSQL for future scaling.

  1. AI Integration

OpenAI API for question explanations

Rather than building a custom NLP model, the system uses OpenAI API to generate high-quality explanations efficiently. The cost will be considerably low because AI calls only occur in question creation. API keys are stored in environment variables for security.

  1. Deployment Strategy

Render as Deployment approach

Lightweight deployment reduces hosting complexity and cost.

  1. Testing & CI/CD

Pytest + GitHub Actions

Continuous integration is implemented via .github/workflows/ci.yml. Each commit triggers backend unit tests with Pytest and coverage reporting to ensure consistent quality.

  1. Scalability & Maintainability

Layered modular architecture

Each Django app encapsulates a specific domain (user, questions, attempts, leaderboard, adminpanel). This modular design facilitates future extensions (e.g., OAuth SSO, analytics dashboards).

Coding 

Standards

  • Branching: main (deployable), feature/*, fix/*; squash-merge with meaningful commit messages.

  • Reviews: every PR requires one reviewer; checklist: naming, tests, error handling, security concerns (CSRF/permissions).

  • Lint/format: Prettier/ESLint (frontend); Black/isort/flake8 (backend).

  • Secrets: .env files ignored; README documents required env keys without values.

Representative improvements

  • Extracted repeated validation into helpers/decorators.

  • Consolidated API error responses to a small set of structured error shapes.

  • Reduced coupling by moving rating aggregation into a dedicated service function.

Testing 

We did not run broad user studies or collect large-scale metrics. Given time and scope, our testing focused on repeatable developer checks and basic automation.

What we have now

Frontend Testing:

Our frontend testing primarily relied on manual testing to verify the visual design and user interface behaviour. Since the most critical logic and data processing were handled in the backend, which underwent different types of automated testing, we focused our frontend testing on ensuring usability, layout consistency, and interactive experience rather than implementing automated test for frontend.

Backend Testing:

  • Backend unit tests (selected modules): question creation rules; attempt evaluation logic; review permissions.

  • Integration tests (API level): happy-path for core flows (create → review → attempt); basic negative cases (missing fields, role mismatch).

  • Manual smoke tests before deployment: critical flows on the deployed site (login, create, attempt, review).

What we do not have yet

  • end-to-end testing using playwrite

  • Full end-to-end browser automation.

  • Performance benchmarks and load testing.

  • Formal usability tests with a cohort.

  • Simultaneous use of over 500 students (only tested with small number of devices simultaneously.)

Near-term plan (post-submission friendly)

  • Add API tests for comments/ratings and pagination.

  • Introduce minimal Playwright suite (attempt flow).

  • Record a lightweight “test matrix” in Confluence mapping stories to checks.

Deployment

Questify is deployed entirely on Render, ensuring that both the frontend and backend are accessible through secure HTTPS URLs and continuously updated from the main GitHub branch. The deployment uses Render’s blueprint system (render.yaml), which defines two web services—one for the Django backend and one for the Vite-based React frontend—each configured to run on Render’s free tier.

Backend Deployment

The backend service (questify-backend) runs on Python 3.11, automatically installing dependencies, collecting static files, and applying database migrations before starting Gunicorn with config.wsgi:application behind $PORT. Security configurations are included through environment variables, which enforce HTTPS, CSRF protection, and SameSite cookie policies. Key environment variables include:

  • SECRET_KEY (auto-generated per deploy)

  • DJANGO_ALLOWED_HOSTS and DJANGO_CORS_ALLOWED_ORIGINS set to Render subdomains

  • SESSION_COOKIE_SECURE, CSRF_COOKIE_SECURE, and SECURE_SSL_REDIRECT enabled

  • DATABASE_URL pointing to the managed Render PostgreSQL instance

The backend is deployed at:
🔗 https://questify-backend-wv3e.onrender.com

Database migrations run automatically during each deployment (python manage.py migrate --noinput), and an initial admin account is created via migration script user/0004_create_admin_superuser.py. The password should be changed immediately after the first login, and additional admin addresses can be specified under ADMIN_EMAILS.

Frontend Deployment

The frontend service (questify-frontend) builds the React application using Vite and Node 20, then serves it through npm run preview bound to $PORT. It communicates with the backend via the environment variable:

  • VITE_API_BASE_URL = https://questify-backend-wv3e.onrender.com/api

The frontend is deployed at:
🔗 https://questify-frontend.onrender.com

Both frontend and backend services run under the same Render blueprint, making the deployment reproducible with a single click or a push to the main branch if auto-redeploy is enabled.

Database and Storage

Questify uses a managed Render PostgreSQL database (questify-database) linked to the backend. Data persistence is guaranteed as long as the instance remains active; however, since all services are on the free plan, the database and its data are scheduled for deletion after November 30 2025 unless upgraded.

Static and media files (e.g., user profile photos) are cleared upon redeployment due to ephemeral storage.

Continuous Integration & Testing

Before any deployment, the CI pipeline (defined in .github/workflows/ci.yml) ensures build and test reliability:

  1. Backend CI:

    • Spins up PostgreSQL 15

    • Installs Django dependencies

    • Runs pytest with pytest-django and coverage checks

    • Applies migrations without seed data

  2. Frontend CI:

    • Installs Node 20 dependencies

    • Executes npm run test

    • Builds the production Vite app

Only after both jobs succeed does GitHub Actions trigger deployment via Render deploy hooks (RENDER_DEPLOY_HOOK and FRONTEND_RENDER_DEPLOY_HOOK). Deployments fail fast if these secrets are missing or if tests fail.

Redeployment

Deployment updates are automatic on every merge or push to main. Manual redeployment can be triggered from Render’s dashboard.

System Setup Summary

Questify uses a full-stack architecture consisting of:

  • Backend: Django + Django REST Framework with SQLite (local) and PostgreSQL (Render production).

  • Frontend: React (Vite) application connected via REST API.

  • External API: OpenAI API used for AI-generated explanations.

  • Deployment: Backend on Render, Frontend on Render or GitHub Pages.

Developers can follow the Setup Instructions in README.md to configure environment files (.env), run database migrations, and start both servers.

A working local environment requires Python 3.11 and Node.js 20 LTS.

Ownership & Access

  • Render services: Hosted under the Team 56 Render account.

  • GitHub repository: IT-PROJECT-TEAM56-OOSD-QUESTION-BANK, currently managed by the development team, maintain team will need further request for access.

  • Environment variables: Stored privately in Render and local .env files (excluded from Git).

  • Default admin account: admin@questify.com / 12345678q (seeded by migration script – must be changed after handover).

  • External API key: OPENAI_API_KEY kept in Render environment only.

Common Pitfalls During Setup

  • Using an outdated Node version (< 20) causes Vite build failures.

  • Virtual environment not activated before pip install and migrations.

  • Missing .env files in frontend/backend leading to API URL errors.

  • CSRF/CORS origin mismatch between frontend and backend domains.

  • Local db.sqlite3 must remain git-ignored to prevent data leakage.

Special Considerations & Lessons Learned

Constraints

The project was completed by a five-member student team, four of whom had little to no prior software-development experience.

This skill gap created a steep learning curve in both backend and frontend development, requiring additional time for training and experimentation before productive work could begin.

Time constraints were also significant. The team began from a high-level idea of “making students more engaged” and had to design the entire product concept, architecture, and feature set from scratch. Considerable time was invested in conceptual alignment and prototype exploration before implementation could start.

In addition, client availability was limited due to the teaching team’s busy semester schedule, making it difficult to secure frequent feedback. This necessitated greater internal decision-making and reliance on the team’s own interpretation of user needs.

What Worked

Although the early stages the team were disorganised, adopting the Scrum methodology later quickly brought structure and efficiency.

  • Defined Scrum roles clarified responsibility and improved the team management.

  • Sprint Planning sessions gave the team clearer direction and realistic short-term goals.

  • Sprint Retrospectives encouraged honest reflection and continuous improvement in teamwork.

  • Pair Programming (one member handling frontend and another backend for the same feature) proved highly effective for cross-functional collaboration.

  • Maintaining an API documentation page facilitated smooth integration between backend and frontend modules.

  • Weekly team meetings were held consistently with full attendance, fostering communication and accountability.

  • Task distribution was balanced and realistic, ensuring that each member contributed meaningfully according to their strengths.

These practices collectively helped transform an inexperienced group into a cohesive, agile development team.

Lessons Learned

Through this project, all members gained a deep appreciation for Agile collaboration and the importance of communication and iteration in a software-engineering context.

The team experienced the full development lifecycle from requirements and design to testing and deployment. and each member gained substantial technical skills in Django, React, REST APIs, CI/CD, and team-based version control.

This experience let every team member understand that effective collaboration and disciplined project management are as critical as technical implementation.

What We Would Do Next

Building on the current MVP, several future enhancements are planned to improve scalability, usability, and engagement:

  • Cloud Deployment – Move from Render to AWS or Azure for better scalability and reliability.

  • University SSO Integration – Adopt the University of Melbourne Single Sign-On system to streamline authentication.

  • Gamification and Rewards – Expand beyond points to include badges, streaks, and small tangible incentives for top performers.

  • Leaderboard Enhancements – Introduce multi-metric ranking (accuracy rate, verified questions, most-liked comments).

  • Peer Profile Visibility – Allow students to view each other’s activity for community engagement.

  • AI-Powered Question Recommendation – Recommend questions based on topic mastery and performance history.

  • Improved AI Feedback – Refine OpenAI prompts or adopt fine-tuned educational models.

  • Mobile Responsiveness – Optimise UI for mobile and tablet devices.
